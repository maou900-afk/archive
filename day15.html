New Year's Eve

Family Dinner.

What can I say about New Year's Eve dinner? We had already had several dinners together and spent more time with your parents. In an almost unexpected burst of enthusiasm, your parents wanted to show us your grandmother's white house. That dinner didn't feel like an exam, yet I couldn't relax or talk about those burning issues, of course. The time I spent that day with your family felt like a celebration.

My dear, there is a profound beauty in sitting at the table with the person you love and their family and seeing their gestures repeated in their father's face, or hearing their laughter in their mother's voice. It's like looking at a treasure map. Getting to know your family was understanding that no one is an island. I bet you noticed it too in Colombia. We are all the result of a series of previous affections and mistakes, of fears that never had the chance to surface. When that family opens a place for you, as yours did during those days we spent in Penang, they are somehow telling you that they trust you to take care of the most precious thing they have. It is a moment of immense gratitude, which I keep in my heart, which I keep in these photos, in the hugs and kisses I gave you, the gratitude of being able to contemplate with my own eyes a dinner where words were unnecessary because the gesture of passing you the pork, or the food they love, or asking you how our relationship has come this far, implicitly conveyed a “welcome to our history.” That day we ate typical dishes of the Nyonya culture and I had never tasted anything so different, I never thought that one day I would end up in Penang on New Year's Eve, proud and thrilled to share my life with your family.

A NOTE FROM WHAT I FELT IN PENANG

Hola, Hermosa

I realized something and it happened at a specific moment, one that might seem curious to you but for me was transformative. When we went to your dad brother house, it changed the way I see many things. You see, it might be the language but your father is a person who can sometimes seem a bit reserved in interaction, even a bit imposing. But seeing the house where his brother lives, seeing him talk with his own people, seeing the fish swimming in his tank and the pineapples in his garden, was like turning on a lamp in a dark room.

He was no longer just the man in front of me, the man who gave you life, but the son of so and so and the grandson of so and so, the result of a sum of affections and customs that he was now sharing with me in front of your uncle. I watched his smile, I watched his hands repeated in his brother’s gestures... I saw in his eyes the admiration and respect he feels for him. I felt deeply moved by what was happening before my eyes. That day I felt, in the enthusiasm of his brother’s words, who said I was welcome a second time to share more about his countless trips to China, I knew that my story, that of the Colombian who knows how to dance, who rolls his tongue to pronounce the R, and yours, didn't seem separate at all. That day I felt like just another member of the family. My story entangled with yours forever.

Seeing photos of when you were a little girl, listening to stories of your grandparents who are no longer here, participating in dinner and in your family’s conversations is invaluable. Walking through the hallways of that house was breathing a different air, almost sacred. Although I didn't know your grandmother, I felt you were introducing her to me in every corner, in the way the furniture seems to keep silence or in that meticulous order one breathes in the atmosphere. Seeing your face light up telling anecdotes about her, or pointing out the place where she used to sit, I understood that she hasn't entirely gone. She lives on in the way you treat those you love most, in that sweet stubbornness you have sometimes. Entering there wasn't just visiting a building created in a mix of cultures, it was entering a system of affections perfected over generations, where every object seems to have a secret story that now, generously, you have shared with me. I felt that upon crossing that threshold, her memory welcomed me, allowing me to be a late but sincere witness to the woman who cemented the home where you learned to be who you are.

And then, observing your parents in the quiet of the afternoon, I saw you clearer than ever, as if a veil had been lifted. Loving them, with their differences, with their language that sometimes escapes me and with their rituals, is the most honest and profound way of loving you completely. You are not an island, my love. You are the precious result of all that accumulated affection. It moves me to my soul to think that all that history, all those efforts and days lived by them, the sweat shed, conspired in my favor through time solely so that you would exist, so that you would be just as you are and could coincide with me, back in my short time in Melbourne, in that exact point of the world so far from everything, at the perfect moment. I think about it and I cannot stop feeling like the luckiest man.

I feel the sacred weight of my past and your history. I see you singing in Spanish, enjoying Bad Bunny, and I find myself constantly thinking about the time when Malaysia gave me shelter. Little by little I feel how the pieces are fitting with yours, less rough each time, more familiar. The traditions and our stories are gradually mixing until I no longer know where one ends and the others begin, and there, my love, is where our family has started to grow. We are planting a new garden, made of patches of distant worlds, where our children will tell stories of a grandfather in Penang and of a coffee land on the other side of the sea. Blessed be the history that brought you to my arms, with all its baggage of customs and memories, because today I know that your past is the garden where I want to sow my future forever. And I think of you, of Malaysia, and I feel proud to have you in my life, just as you are.

From the other side of the world with love, Andrés.
